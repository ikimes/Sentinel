## Purpose

This note tests whether the chosen first matter type is commercially dense enough to matter.

The first matter type remains:

`post-intake cross-market safety follow-up matter`

The danger is simple:

the matter can be real, understandable, and still too infrequent or too tolerable to drive buying behavior.

## How To Use This Scorecard

Score a candidate design partner or workflow on each dimension from `1` to `5`.

- `1` means weak commercial signal
- `3` means mixed or uncertain
- `5` means strong commercial signal

The score is not final truth.

It is a forcing function to avoid fooling ourselves with a vivid but low-density pain point.

## Scoring Dimensions

| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Recurrence` | rare edge case | appears monthly or unevenly | happens often enough to shape weekly operations |
| `Operational severity` | annoying but manageable | creates recurring delays or confusion | creates meaningful operational risk or fire drills |
| `Cross-system breadth` | mostly one system | sometimes crosses two systems | repeatedly crosses multiple systems and teams |
| `Clock pressure` | soft internal timing only | occasional due-state ambiguity | hard external or market-driven clocks matter regularly |
| `Evidence fragmentation` | evidence mostly lives in one place | some stitching required | evidence is routinely reconstructed across tools |
| `Manual reconciliation cost` | little manual work | moderate spreadsheet or meeting burden | status is repeatedly rebuilt by humans |
| `Leadership visibility` | leadership barely sees it | visible during exceptions | already recognized as a real operating problem |
| `Buy urgency` | "nice to have" | interesting but deferrable | strong motivation to change current process |
| `Overlay appetite` | wants replacement or nothing | undecided | clearly wants an overlay next to incumbents |
| `Repeatable model fit` | each instance looks different | some shared shape | same core matter model fits repeatedly |

## Interpretation Bands

### `40-50`

Strong candidate.

The workflow is commercially dense enough to pursue seriously if the buyer path is also credible.

### `30-39`

Borderline.

Interesting, but probably still needs sharper qualification or a narrower first proof.

### `Below 30`

Weak candidate.

The workflow may be real, but it is unlikely to sustain a new product motion.

## Hard-Gate Questions

Regardless of score, be cautious if any of these are true:

- recurrence is below `3`
- overlay appetite is below `3`
- repeatable model fit is below `3`

Those are the easiest ways to talk ourselves into a weak pilot.

## Evidence To Collect During Discovery

- approximate number of matters per week or month
- number of teams touched by a typical matter
- number of systems touched by a typical matter
- whether clocks are internal only or authority / market-driven
- how many manual touchpoints are required to understand current status
- whether recent near-misses, escalations, or audit pain exist
- whether leadership has already asked for a better control surface

## Good-Fit Pattern

A strong Sentinel candidate usually sounds like this:

- this matter happens often enough to be familiar
- multiple teams already know the workflow is messy
- the workaround uses spreadsheets, email, or recurring meetings
- the same types of ownership and due-state questions recur each time
- the buyer can describe the pain without inventing a new story for every example

## Weak-Fit Pattern

A weak Sentinel candidate usually sounds like this:

- the examples are dramatic but rare
- every example requires different logic
- the pain is mostly storytelling rather than operating risk
- leadership likes the concept, but does not care enough to change tools
- the real ask turns into reporting, case processing, or general workflow modernization

## Practical Decision Rule

Do not treat the first matter type as validated until at least one real prospect scores strongly on:

- recurrence
- operational severity
- overlay appetite
- repeatable model fit

If those stay soft, the wedge may remain plausible but commercially thin.
