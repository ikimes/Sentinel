## Purpose

This rubric operationalizes the overlay buying-path question for real prospect conversations.

It should be used only after the workflow itself already looks promising.

Its job is to answer:

- can this prospect actually buy a narrow overlay
- who must say yes
- who can say no
- what framing makes the first purchase legible

## When To Use It

Use this rubric only when all of these are already at least `yellow`:

- one recurring matter type is clear
- the workflow crosses systems and teams
- the pain is operational, not only analytical
- the prospect has some interest in an overlay rather than immediate replacement

If those are not true yet, use the broader discovery guide first.

## Core Rule

Do not let this conversation drift into:

- future platform ambition
- broad AI transformation
- full suite replacement
- generic workflow modernization

Keep the conversation anchored to one narrow overlay next to incumbent systems.

## What The Rubric Must Resolve

By the end of the conversation, we should be able to answer:

- who feels the pain weekly
- who can sponsor a pilot
- what budget a first pilot would come from
- who must approve an overlay
- who is most likely to block it
- what first commercial wrapper feels smallest and safest
- why the buyer would choose overlay rather than extension or internal build

## Interview Flow

### 1. Confirm The Workflow Is Worth Buying Around

Goal:

make sure the buying-path conversation stays attached to a real operational pain, not a generic product conversation.

Questions:

- Which one recurring follow-up workflow would justify new spend if improved?
- How often does that workflow create status meetings, spreadsheet tracking, or manual follow-up?
- When leadership asks for status, what is hardest to answer quickly?
- What makes this workflow feel like more than a reporting annoyance?

Strong signal:

- the sponsor can describe one recurring workflow that already feels operationally embarrassing, risky, or costly

Weak signal:

- the sponsor likes the concept but cannot point to one workflow that clearly deserves budget

### 2. Identify The Operational Sponsor

Goal:

separate enthusiastic users from someone who could actually champion a pilot.

Questions:

- Who would personally sponsor the first pilot?
- Who would define success in operational terms?
- Who would be accountable if the pilot did not happen and the workflow stayed messy?
- Who has the credibility to say this is a real control problem rather than a local inconvenience?

Strong signal:

- one named operations or systems leader clearly owns the pain and the pilot story

Weak signal:

- several people care, but no one clearly owns the pilot motion

### 3. Identify The Budget Path

Goal:

find the actual funding path, not just general support.

Questions:

- If this pilot were approved, what budget would it most likely come from?
- Has this team bought adjacent overlay, workflow, or control software before?
- Would this be framed as operations improvement, systems optimization, inspection readiness, or something else?
- What internal event makes budget easier to unlock for this kind of problem?

Strong signal:

- one plausible budget source is named without hand-waving

Weak signal:

- the sponsor says the problem matters, but budget is abstract or hypothetical

### 4. Map The Approval Path

Goal:

understand who must accept a narrow overlay beside incumbent systems.

Questions:

- Who must approve an overlay next to the incumbent stack?
- Who owns the incumbent platform Sentinel would sit beside?
- Which architecture or security concerns would surface first?
- What would make those stakeholders comfortable with the overlay boundary?

Strong signal:

- approvers and their concerns are legible, and the overlay boundary reduces perceived risk

Weak signal:

- the approval path is unclear or politically hostile

### 5. Test Overlay Appetite Versus Alternatives

Goal:

make sure the buyer truly wants an overlay and is not defaulting to it only because nothing else is fully defined.

Questions:

- Why should this be an overlay rather than one more workflow screen in the incumbent suite?
- Why should this not be built internally on current workflow platforms?
- What would make a narrow overlay feel safer than a broader replacement project?
- If the first purchase were small, what would make it worth doing anyway?

Strong signal:

- the buyer can articulate why overlay is the right shape, not just the convenient placeholder

Weak signal:

- the buyer keeps falling back to suite extension, reporting, or internal build as the "real" answer

### 6. Test The First Commercial Wrapper

Goal:

find the smallest first offer that still points to the real product.

Questions:

- Which description makes the first purchase easiest to justify:
  - narrow operational control overlay
  - evidence-grade follow-up workspace
  - exception control surface
- Which framing sounds too big or too vague internally?
- Which framing best matches the budget owner and approval path?
- Which framing preserves the real product rather than hollowing it out?

Strong signal:

- one framing clearly lowers friction without changing the product center

Weak signal:

- the only buyable frame turns Sentinel into a different business

## Capture Template

Record the conversation in this structure:

- `qualified workflow`
- `operational sponsor`
- `weekly users`
- `budget owner`
- `budget source`
- `approval stakeholders`
- `likely blocker`
- `overlay boundary concerns`
- `best first commercial framing`
- `strongest alternative to Sentinel`
- `why overlay might win`
- `why overlay might fail`

## Scorecard

Score each dimension from `1` to `5`.

- `Workflow visibility to leadership`
  - `1`: leadership barely notices
  - `5`: leadership already sees it as a recurring control problem
- `Sponsor clarity`
  - `1`: no clear pilot sponsor
  - `5`: one named sponsor with direct ownership
- `Budget clarity`
  - `1`: no plausible budget source
  - `5`: one named budget path already feels credible
- `Approval clarity`
  - `1`: approval path is unknown or hostile
  - `5`: approval stakeholders and concerns are already legible
- `Overlay appetite`
  - `1`: buyer really wants extension or replacement
  - `5`: buyer clearly prefers a narrow overlay
- `Incumbent resistance`
  - `1`: incumbent owner likely blocks
  - `5`: incumbent owner concerns seem manageable
- `Commercial wrapper fit`
  - `1`: no first framing feels buyable
  - `5`: one framing clearly lowers buying friction without hollowing out the product

### Total Interpretation

- `29-35`
  - strong overlay buying-path candidate
- `21-28`
  - promising but fragile; more qualification needed
- `20 or below`
  - overlay path is weak; do not treat as near-term proof

## Green / Yellow / Red Readout

### Green

- one sponsor, one budget path, one approval path, and one acceptable commercial frame are all visible

### Yellow

- the workflow qualifies, but budget or approval still feels fuzzy

### Red

- the workflow is real, but nobody can connect sponsor, budget, approval, and overlay rationale into one buying motion

## Exit Decision

Advance only if the prospect can answer these clearly:

- who sponsors the pilot
- who pays for it
- who approves it
- why overlay is the right shape
- what first frame makes the purchase legible

If any two of those remain weak, the buying path is still not earned.
